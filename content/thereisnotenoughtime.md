Time has a constant presence in my work — not as a concept to be understood, but as something to be felt.

We rarely stop to consider how time, and our use of it, is the foundation on which our existence and our civilizations are built. We are part of an endlessly changing reality, yet we seem incapable of perceiving that change as it happens. Can we feel time passing? Can we witness reality transforming around us?

This study confronts those questions through the two senses we rely on most: vision and hearing. A sequence of satellite images documents the transformation of Earth's surface over 30 years — the cumulative imprint of human activity made visible from space. Alongside it, the sound space renders time in its most elemental form: second by second, incessant and indifferent.

Together they create a collision. A few seconds of sound contain decades of planetary change. Thirty years become an irreversible instant. In that gap — between what we hear and what we see — lies the work's central question: how much is reality changing around us, and within us, without us ever noticing?

-----
 
Satellite imagery from the Landsat-5 Mission (1984–2012) forms the visual backbone of the work — decades of Earth's surface change, collected through geemap**, a Python package for Google Earth Engine. All imagery is sourced from U.S. Geological Survey public domain datasets. Sounds were designed in MaxMSP and then used for the composition inside Ableton Live. Landsat datasets are federally created data and therefore reside in the public domain and may be used, transferred, or reproduced without copyright restriction.
